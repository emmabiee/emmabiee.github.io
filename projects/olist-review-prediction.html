Olist Negative-Review Prediction

An end-to-end machine-learning early-warning system that predicts which Olist e-commerce orders will receive a negative (1–2 star) customer review — so the Customer-Experience team can step in before the customer is unhappy.

The Problem

Olist is a Brazilian e-commerce marketplace that connects thousands of small sellers to large online storefronts. After every delivery, the customer leaves a 1–5 star review — and those reviews are public, drive seller reputation, and are a leading indicator of churn.

A negative review (1–2 stars) is costly, and by the time it is written the damage is already done: roughly one delivered order in eight receives one. Rather than being handed a fixed brief, I had to define my own problem from the raw dataset. The one I chose: predict, at or shortly after an order is placed, which orders are at high risk of a negative review — so the Customer-Experience team can intervene proactively (expedite a slow shipment, send a proactive apology, offer a goodwill voucher) instead of reacting after the fact.

Key Findings

Late delivery is the dominant driver

When an order arrives after its promised date, the negative-review rate climbs sharply — and keeps climbing the later it is. In the final model, delivery_vs_estimate_days (actual minus estimated delivery date) is the single most important feature by a wide margin, dwarfing every other signal. The clearest lever the business has is simply keeping its delivery promise.

Negative reviews are a costly minority

Only 12.8% of delivered orders receive a 1–2 star review — common enough to hurt seller reputation, rare enough that plain accuracy is misleading (predicting "always positive" already scores ~87%). The project is therefore evaluated on PR-AUC with class-weighted models and a stratified split.

Shipping-cost perception measurably hurts

Orders with a high freight-to-price ratio — where delivery feels expensive relative to the goods themselves — draw harsher reviews. It is a concrete, actionable lever for the pricing team: freight subsidies or clearer shipping-cost messaging where the ratio is extreme.

Operational complexity raises risk

Orders split across multiple sellers, and those with longer carrier-handover and transit times, review worse — consistent with "more moving parts, more chances to disappoint." Category and destination state add secondary signal, much of it acting through delivery time.

Methodology

1
Data Quality Audit
Profiled an 11-table SQLite database (~99K orders). Diagnosed missing delivery timestamps as undelivered orders, de-duplicated reviews, and fixed the dataset's known column-name typos.
2
Exploratory Analysis
Built an order-level base table by aggregating nine normalised tables, then tested each hypothesised driver against the negative-review rate with bivariate analysis and visualisations.
3
Feature Engineering
Engineered 35 leakage-free features (delivery timeline, freight ratio, order composition, payment, product, geography) — explicitly excluding anything generated by the review itself.
4
Preprocessing Pipeline
A scikit-learn ColumnTransformer imputes, scales and one-hot-encodes with rare-level pooling — fitted inside the model pipeline so test statistics never leak into training.
5
Model Comparison
Three models — Logistic Regression, Random Forest and HistGradientBoosting — cross-validated with stratified k-fold and ranked on PR-AUC; the best is selected automatically.
6
Evaluation & Delivery
Held-out test scoring (PR-AUC, ROC-AUC, precision/recall, confusion matrix, permutation importance), a decision threshold tuned on out-of-fold predictions, and a risk-ranked CSV of every order.

What I Learned

Preventing Target Leakage

A review only exists after delivery, so every feature had to be restricted to information available at order time. Auditing and excluding leakage columns was the single most important — and easiest to get wrong — part of the build.

Metrics for Imbalanced Data

With only ~13% positives, accuracy flatters a useless model. Leading with PR-AUC, applying class weighting, and tuning the decision threshold on out-of-fold predictions kept the evaluation honest.

Engineering a Reusable Pipeline

Instead of a single notebook, the solution is a config-driven src/ package with automated tests and CI. A new experiment is a one-line YAML edit, not a code rewrite.

From Prediction to Decision

A model is only useful if someone acts on it. Framing the output as a risk-ranked queue for the Customer-Experience team turned a probability into an operational tool.
