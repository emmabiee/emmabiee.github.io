Electoral College: Why the Map Deceives

Geography doesn't vote — people do. The map above is dominated by red because Republican-leaning states cover more land area. But electoral votes are allocated by population, so a single blue state like California (54 EV) outweighs a dozen small red states combined. The bar below shows the actual electoral vote split.

1

2024 Broke the Popular Vote Pattern

Republicans won both the popular vote and the Electoral College for the first time since 2004, with Donald Trump surpassing 91 million votes — an unprecedented total for any Republican candidate.

2

Turnout Explosion

199 million votes were cast in 2024, up 89% from 2000's 105 million. Both parties drove historic turnout — Democrats hit 85.9M and Republicans reached 91.4M, both all-time records.

3

Florida's Red Shift

Florida went from the most razor-thin margin in modern history (0.0% in 2000) to a commanding −13.1% Republican margin in 2024, transforming from the ultimate swing state into a solidly red stronghold.

4

The Blue Wall Crumbled

Pennsylvania, Michigan, and Wisconsin — the so-called "Blue Wall" — all flipped Republican in 2024 after going Democratic in 2020. These three states had been reliable Democratic territory for decades before 2016.

5

County-Level Realignment

102 counties flipped from Democrat to Republican between 2020 and 2024, while only 15 went the other direction — a nearly 7-to-1 ratio signalling a broad geographic shift toward the GOP.

6

Loyalty Runs Deep

36 states voted for the same party in all 7 elections from 2000 to 2024 — 12 solidly Democratic and 20 solidly Republican, plus DC. Only 15 states swung between parties during this quarter-century period.
