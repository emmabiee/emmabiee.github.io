Executive Summary

In 2022, 1 in 5 U.S. flights arrived late — 549,942 delays out of 2.7 million arrivals, averaging 67 minutes per incident. The root causes are largely within industry control: carrier operations and late-arriving aircraft together account for 72% of all delays, while weather and security — factors airlines cannot control — make up just 4%. Delay severity concentrates at small regional airports where a single disruption cascades through limited schedules, whereas high-volume hubs like ATL and DFW maintain shorter average delays through operational resilience. Among major carriers, Southwest leads in absolute delay count (20% of all delays), but regional operators Mesa and SkyWest suffer the longest per-flight delays. These patterns suggest that targeted investment in carrier scheduling, crew management, and aircraft turnaround processes would yield the greatest reduction in passenger disruption.

Recommendations for Reducing Flight Delays

Based on the 2022 delay data, the following actions target the highest-impact, most controllable causes of passenger disruption.

Recommendation 1
Invest in carrier operations and turnaround efficiency
Carrier-caused delays (38% of total) are the single largest category. Faster boarding, better crew scheduling, and predictive maintenance would directly cut the top delay driver.
Recommendation 2
Break the late-aircraft cascade at regional hubs
Late aircraft (33%) propagate through tight regional schedules. Adding schedule buffers at spoke airports and pre-positioning spare aircraft at high-risk hubs would limit cascading delays.
Recommendation 3
Address NAS bottlenecks at congested corridors
National Airspace System delays (25%) stem from air traffic control and infrastructure constraints. Modernising ground-delay programs at FL/TX/CA hubs would improve flow management.
Recommendation 4
Bolster weather resilience for May-July peak
Weather delays spike in late spring/early summer, not winter. Pre-positioning de-icing and convective weather decision tools for the May-July window would smooth the seasonal bump.
Recommendation 5
Support regional carriers with post-pandemic recovery resources
Mesa and SkyWest show the highest per-flight delays, and JetBlue's average rose 35% since 2020. Pilot pipeline and fleet utilisation programs would help close the gap.
Recommendation 6
Monitor small-airport delay outliers separately
Airports like Valdosta (250 min avg) appear extreme but handle very few flights. Separate reporting for airports below a minimum traffic threshold prevents misleading rankings.
