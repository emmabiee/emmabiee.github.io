UK Gender Pay Gap Analysis

SQL-driven analysis of 10,174 UK employers — revealing that the pay gap is primarily a workforce composition issue, not a pay discrimination problem, with larger companies showing what structured policies can achieve.

The Problem

The UK mandates that employers with 250+ employees report their gender pay gap annually. But the headline figures — often reduced to a single percentage — can be misleading without context. A 100% gap at a football club doesn't mean the same thing as a 15% gap at a bank. The goal: use SQL to dig beneath the surface of 10,174 company filings, separate structural patterns from noise, and produce findings that could inform policy decisions rather than just generate headlines.

Key Findings

9.8% median gap — men earn more across 78% of companies

Using the median of median hourly pay gaps (more robust than the mean, which is skewed by outliers in a 375-point range), men are paid 9.8% more per hour than women at the typical UK employer. Only 13% of companies report a female-favoured gap.

Pay gap decreases as company size increases

Companies with 250-499 employees average a 12.8% gap, while those with 20,000+ employees average 9.7%. Formal pay structures, transparent salary scales, public accountability, and active diversity policies — all more common in larger organisations — appear to narrow the gap.

Large gaps do not equal unfair pay

Football clubs and construction firms top the list at 100% — but this reflects male-dominated workforces (elite athletes vs admin staff), not pay discrimination for equal roles. The data is more meaningful when filtered by company size and analysed by sector using SIC codes.

The "leaky pipeline" — women are 55% of bottom roles, 40% of top

Quartile analysis reveals the structural root of the pay gap: women are over-represented in the lowest-paid quartile and progressively under-represented as pay increases. This pattern is consistent across all company sizes, pointing to systemic barriers in career progression rather than unequal pay for equal work.

Analysis Approach

1
Data Quality Assessment
Identified 361 late submissions, 3,700 missing URLs, and excluded four bonus columns with >25% ambiguous zeros.
2
National Pay Gap
Calculated the 9.8% median using PERCENTILE_CONT, justified median over mean, mapped the direction breakdown.
3
Company Size Analysis
Grouped by employer size bands to reveal the inverse size-gap relationship. Attributed to structural and policy differences.
4
Extreme Companies
Identified the most skewed employers with DENSE_RANK, then filtered by size to surface the most impactful cases.
5
Sector Analysis
Mapped SIC codes to sectors. Education (25.7%) and construction (24.3%) lead; security is the only female-favoured sector.
6
Workforce Quartiles
Analysed female representation across pay quartiles by company size — revealing the consistent "leaky pipeline" pattern.

SQL Techniques Used

All queries use PostgreSQL syntax, showcasing intermediate-to-advanced SQL patterns:

-- Robust median calculation with outlier context SELECT MAX(diffmedianhourlypercent) AS highest_male_bias, MIN(diffmedianhourlypercent) AS highest_female_bias, PERCENTILE_CONT(0.5) WITHIN GROUP (ORDER BY diffmedianhourlypercent) AS median_pay_gap FROM gender_pay_gap_21_22 WHERE diffmedianhourlypercent IS NOT NULL;

Key patterns include PERCENTILE_CONT for median calculation, DENSE_RANK for tied rankings, CASE expressions for categorisation, HAVING for minimum sample sizes, and SIC code parsing with LEFT() for sector grouping.

What I Learned

Context Changes Everything

A 100% pay gap at a football club and a 15% gap at a bank tell completely different stories. Learning to question surface-level statistics and dig into the structural reasons behind them was the most valuable takeaway.

Data Quality Comes First

The bonus columns looked usable until I checked — 25%+ zeros with no way to distinguish missing from genuine. This taught me to always audit data quality before building any analysis on top of it.

Metric Selection Matters

Choosing median over mean wasn't arbitrary — with a 375-point range, the mean would be distorted by outliers. Justifying this choice explicitly is something I'll carry into every future analysis.

Policy-Ready Analysis

Moving from "here are the numbers" to "here's what should change" pushed me to think about who would use these findings and what decisions they'd need to make.
