Executive Summary

Across 10,174 UK employers, men are paid a median 9.8% more per hour than women. 78% of companies report a male-favoured pay gap, while only 13% favour women — the remaining 9% report zero gap. The gap is not primarily about unequal pay for equal work. It reflects structural workforce composition: women are over-represented in lower-pay quartiles (55% of lowest-paid roles) and under-represented in top quartiles (40%). Company size matters — larger firms report smaller gaps, likely due to formal pay structures, public accountability, and diversity policies. Sectors with the widest gaps (education, construction, financial services) are driven by occupational segregation, not pay discrimination. Policy efforts should focus on workforce composition and progression pathways rather than headline gap figures alone.

Important Caveats

What the gender pay gap is — and what it is not.

Pay gap is not equal pay

The pay gap measures workforce composition differences, not whether men and women are paid equally for the same role. A company can have a large gap with zero pay discrimination if men dominate senior roles.

Self-reported, unaudited data

Companies self-report their figures to the Gender Pay Gap Service. There is no independent audit, making the data only as reliable as each employer's internal calculation.

No weighting by company size

A firm with 250 employees counts the same as one with 25,000. This means the national average reflects a typical company, not a typical employee experience.

Missing context

No part-time/full-time split, no seniority or role data, no demographic breakdown. The gap figure alone cannot identify root causes without this additional context.

Recommendations for Improving the Data and Reducing the Gap

Based on the analysis, these actions would improve both data quality and structural outcomes.

Data Quality 1
Mandate exact employee counts by gender
Current size bands (250-499, etc.) prevent employee-weighted national averages. Exact headcounts would allow weighting larger employers proportionally.
Data Quality 2
Require role and seniority reporting
Without role data, we can't distinguish structural gaps from pay discrimination. Adding job-level breakdowns would enable like-for-like comparisons.
Data Quality 3
Fix bonus data reporting
Over 25% of bonus fields are zero — but zero could mean no bonus, no gap, or missing data. Clearer categories would make bonus analysis viable.
Policy 1
Focus on progression, not just pay
The quartile data shows women are 55% of bottom roles but 40% of top roles. Closing the gap requires better promotion pipelines and flexible senior roles.
Policy 2
Target sector-specific interventions
Education (25.7% gap) and construction (24.3%) need different solutions — one needs senior leadership balance, the other needs workforce entry pathways.
Policy 3
Replicate what large firms do well
The inverse size-gap relationship shows that formal pay structures and transparency work. Extending these practices to mid-size firms could narrow gaps at scale.
