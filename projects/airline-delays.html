FAA Airline Delays Analysis

Power BI dashboard analysing 20 years of U.S. flight delay data — uncovering which causes are controllable, which carriers are worsening, and where targeted investment would reduce the most passenger disruption.

The Problem

Flight delays cost the U.S. airline industry billions annually and affect millions of passengers, but not all delays are created equal. Some are within carrier control (crew scheduling, maintenance, turnaround processes), while others stem from external factors (weather, air traffic control, security). Without separating these categories, stakeholders can't prioritise the interventions that would actually reduce disruption. The goal: build a multi-page Power BI dashboard that surfaces the most actionable patterns from 20 years of Bureau of Transportation Statistics data.

Key Findings

72% of delays are within industry control — not weather

Carrier operations (38%) and late-arriving aircraft (33%) together account for nearly three-quarters of all delays. Weather and security — the causes airlines cannot control — make up just 4%. This means the majority of passenger disruption is addressable through better scheduling, maintenance, and turnaround processes.

Small regional airports face the worst delays per flight

Valdosta Regional averages over 4 hours per delayed flight — but handles very few flights. Low-traffic airports lack schedule buffers, so a single delayed inbound cascades through the day. Major hubs like ATL and DFW maintain shorter average delays through operational resilience and fleet redundancy.

Delays have worsened across most carriers since 2020

The post-pandemic traffic recovery outpaced staffing and fleet readiness. JetBlue's average delay rose 35% (60 to 81 minutes) from 2020 to 2022. SkyWest consistently sits above 80 minutes. Only Southwest kept delays relatively stable despite carrying the highest absolute delay count.

May leads weather delays — summer storms outpace winter

Convective storms and severe weather in late spring create more delays than winter snow events. This counter-intuitive finding suggests weather resilience investments should target the May–July window, not just the winter months most people associate with travel disruptions.

Power BI Techniques

The dashboard uses a range of Power BI features to deliver interactive, insight-driven analysis:

-- Controllable delay percentage (DAX measure) Controllable % = DIVIDE( SUM( delays[carrier_ct] ) + SUM( delays[late_aircraft_ct] ), SUM( delays[arr_del15] ), 0 ) -- Dynamic title based on slicer selection Chart Title = "Top 5 States — " & SELECTEDVALUE( delays[year], "All Years" )

Key techniques include calculated measures for controllable vs external delay ratios, dynamic titles driven by slicer selections, conditional formatting on KPI cards, bookmarks for page navigation, and drill-through from state-level to airport-level detail.

What I Learned

Insight-First Design

Chart titles should tell the story, not describe the chart. "72% of delays are controllable" communicates more than "Delay Causes Breakdown." This principle transformed the dashboard from a data display into a decision tool.

Controllable vs External Framing

Grouping delay causes into actionable categories changed the entire narrative. Instead of five separate slices, stakeholders see one clear message: most delays are fixable through operational investment.

Volume vs Severity Trap

Southwest leads in total delays because it flies the most routes — not because it's the worst performer. Presenting count and average side-by-side prevents misleading conclusions from volume-driven rankings.

DAX for Dynamic Storytelling

Using DAX measures for calculated KPIs, dynamic titles, and conditional formatting turned static visuals into an interactive narrative that adapts to the user's exploration path.
